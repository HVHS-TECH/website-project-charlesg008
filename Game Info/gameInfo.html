What is

?

Pump It Up, developed and published in 1999 by South Korean game company Andamiro, is a rhythm-based dance arcade game where players step on directional arrows in time with the music.

Unlike DanceDanceRevolution, which uses a four-arrow layout, Pump It Up features five-panels on each dance pad with diagonal arrows and a center button, encouraging more dynamic and expressive footwork through a wider range of step combinations.

Though it occupies a more niche corner of the rhythm game genre, it has built a loyal global following thanks to its energetic K-pop tracks, challenging step charts, and competitive scene.

One of its standout formats is doubles play, where a single player uses both pads to perform step charts that demand more stamina, precision, and spatial awareness. Over the years, Pump It Up has evolved through multiple versions and international tournaments, remaining a favorite among rhythm game enthusiasts.

How to play

Overview

This short section will go over helping with how to start playing, with some guidance of what to look out for on levels 1 through to 10.

The Basics

Pump It Up is a rhythm game played on one to two five-panel dance pads: up-left, up-right, center, down-left, and down-right.
Singles play (one pad) on levels 1–5 typically introduce single steps and straightforward patterns, helping players build coordination and confidence. Arrows scroll upward on screen, and players step on the matching panels as they reach the top.
Make sure to use a different foot for each different arrow that appears(this will come in handy with understannding patterns later.) These early levels will focus on basic timing, simple rhythms, and familiarizing yourself with the pad layout.

Twists & Turns

As you reach Singles levels 6–10, charts begin to include what are colloquially called twists.
These include directional changes, crossing over your legs (reaching over or behind a leg with the other,) that require smoother transitions and weight shifts.
These levels also have higher densities of notes (typically 4th & 8th notes.)
These charts encourage players to think ahead and move with rhythm, rather than reacting step-by-step.

Auto Velocity Advantage

To support readability and timing, PiU includes tools in the Command Menu like AV (Auto Velocity).
AV helps separate dense note clusters and make patterns easier to follow as charts become more visually complex by setting the scroll of the notes to a constant BPM.

Double the Challenge

If you're exploring Doubles mode, where the player uses all 10 panels, charts often emphasize middle-4 and middle-6 patterns.
These layouts focus on the shared center and nearby diagonals across both pads, making heavy use of twists, and may cause confusion at first.
The best thing to help understand them is to remember to always use a different foot for each arrow.
